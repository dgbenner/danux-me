DroneUp

Drone Delivery Logistics


DroneUp is a Virginia-based airspace solutions and software company. Founded by former military logistics, technology and operations-experts they are actively solving 'last mile' challenges for retailers, as well as operating an on-demand marketplace of over 10,000 FAA-certified pilots.

With its Fortune 1-partner Walmart, DroneUp currently offers drone delivery in six states (AZ, AR, FL, TX, UT, VA). I was involved in teams such as: E-Commerce Platforms (lead), Product Flight Compliance, Vendor Portal (lead to product owner steward), Walmart.com Integration, Research Ops (lead).


E-Commerce Design

A suite of customer-facing e-commerce applications integrates with 'hub operations'—physical locations with drones, flight hardware and software, packing and delivery capabilities.

The vendor or merchant is a fluid idea in 'last mile'. Primarily, all hubs are on Walmart property, and deliver Walmart purchases. However, DroneUp looks to expand their clients to Doordash, Instacart, Uber Eats and residential communities to build partnerships for a hyper-local delivery network.

DroneUp also partners with more 'backstage' or operations companies like Olo, Square or Toast to share merchant or point-of-sale transactions. Additionally, DroneUp also spun up its own e-commerce platform like Doordash—although the potential for growth is limited because the direct-to-consumer market is flooded with loss leaders.

Research

When I joined DroneUp there was no research or contact with users or pilots, no buyer profiles, metrics, or usability tools worked into the process.

The live web experience was built on 'Woo Commerce', an 'out of the box' Wordpress platform. My first test drive I realized most customers within range were not receiving any UI feedback if the hub was down for weather, maintenance, network issues or they were outside of operating hours. My first initiative was to get users feedback about why they couldn't get a delivery. After that, I cleared as many roadblocks as I could to get them to complete the purchase, even if immediate delivery was unavailable for the moment.

I'd created multiple low- or no-cost proposals for field research, interviews and moderated testing. I'd advocated for these tools weekly to the UX Director.

Ultimately, I wasn't able to get budget or permission for usability or other research, so I did moderated testing with new employees.

Working with the customer service lead, I did find some customer complaints and questions were catalogued through a limited chat tool (Zoho Chat). I started tracking, pulling themes and direct quotes and reporting in a monthly cadence. It proved to be enlightening to the E-com team and senior leadership—for instance, the general public expected real time responses with the drone pilot much like the expectation set for apps like UberEats (which we were nowhere near that level of sophistication).

I'd also developed a relationship with the marketing director and was able to piggyback usability questions into her market research. I'd also uncovered a metrics tool included in the V1 Woo Commerce platform. The data tracked sales and a good deal of customer data, however at least 60-70% of our customers were DroneUp or Walmart employees testing the systems heavily skewing our metrics. I worked with a data scientist to start purging their work and personal email addresses to get more accurate results—which was a big win to start pulling proper metrics.

Vendor Portal

To scale properly DroneUp needs fly 300-500 missions per hub, per day. (In 2023, this was about 20-30 missions collectively from all hubs, per month). Each vendor or merchant has their own pricing, picking, packing and flight rules.

DroneUp itself uses a very technical application called HubOps. However for a restauranteur or a Walmart store manager, we needed to build a platform for them to add stores, products, pricing, and help both the vendor and DroneUp converse about orders and statuses. And above all, give the vendors visibility to see revenue and control their delivery margins.

I was fortunate enough to work with a visionary in 'last mile' who had understood the 'white glove' service we wanted to bring our vendors. Unfortunately, he, the lead engineer and the API-lead were let go— and no one in the company had any idea about the upcoming need of this platform for Walmart or any client we'd take on.

I'd been iterating wireframes, key visuals and working collaboratively with the Director of Ecommerce but only for a short time. I understood the urgency and value, and worked aggressively to explain and sell this necessity into upper management. I'd presented this concept to tech leadership, C-suite, marketing and customer service to illustrate the need until a team was finally assigned.
