EY

EY Lens Platform


EY (Ernst and Young) is a London-based global "professional services" company with over 350,000 employees. As one of the "Big Four" accounting firms, they provide accounting, assurance (auditing and analysis), and transactional services (e.g., mergers and acquisitions).


Employee Overview

With their advanced education and expertise, EY's client-facing employees serve clients with highest degree of professionalism, and are entrusted with some of the company’s most valuable information.

They excel in many different fields and work with a broad range of countries and cultures worldwide—confidently handling international assignments that involve complex government regulations and tax laws.


The Situation

EY directors and strategists aim to develop their clients’ front line business (the businesses they’re directly involved with). EY utilizes an in-house customer relationship management system called 'EY Mercury,' designed to capture client names, contact information, connections, and titles, along with client activity, open jobs, and project details.

However, this system, which is over 20 years old, has become extremely challenging to use. To address this, EY has developed a secondary application called 'EY Lens' that 'sits on top of' the legacy Mercury system. Its purpose is to provide a more user-friendly experience similar to Salesforce, reducing the need for extensive data entry and showcasing potential sales opportunities or 'pipeline opportunities'.

Both Mercury and Lens run on data from the "front line", and that critical data simply isn't there. "Front line" professionals avoid both systems—making identifying new sales opportunities or fact-base company strategies nearly impossible.


Objective

Find out why "front line" professionals are not engaging or adopting the applications EY Mercury and EY Lens.

Findings

We collected 1200+ data points from more than 40 hours of interviews and discovered a clear misunderstanding existed between the "front line" professionals and the stakeholders. In the middle, was the EY Digital Strategy Team trying to build a useful application.

The EY Digital Strategy Team had banked on the technology and capabilities in their immediate budget and toolkit—creating a business logic-based services recommender engine under the guise of being AI-Assisted that would suggest similar projects, teams, or services for front-line professionals to sell to their clients.

We continually level-set we were on a exploration / discovery, and concept testing their recommendation engine would be a facet of the project.

Ultimately, the EY Digital Strategy Team was disappointed we didn't get any positive responses from concept testing. Users found it too simplistic compared to their complex client relationships.


Outcomes

The discovery revealed that the challenges in user adoption of both EY Mercury and EY Lens were rooted in computer-based miscommunication by: inconsistent use of values or metrics, no instructional guidelines or assistance from the system, consistent task failure, duplicate job codes, or lack of a common syntax for job names. In some cases their end client's company would shift their organizational names quarterly—making over 400+ business entities requiring updating or reconcilation in the EY system. Fortunately some data-related items could be remedied with tooltips, more concrete values and "in-application" documentation.

The end users had very little or nothing to gain by engaging with the application or strategic leadership. And the more they tried or used the system, the more drag it would create on serving their clients. Incentivizing or giving them a roadmap on how to be a director would help them see the value in creating and managing their own sales pipeline.

A pivot to the idea of cultivating a better sales culture and educating the front line on sales technique would likely be out of scope for their digital product-focused stakeholder team—even though I believe that would have been a more advisable direction.

Finally, the discovery made clear to our stakeholders the importance of involving end users in the development process and the value of the Recommender Engine. And any time people in leadership understand the value of the user in the design/build process is always a "win" in my book.

Challenges

Initially the client shared information about their company hierarchy, structure and goals through very dense infographics and slides. They had done some personas, research, focus groups and affinity mapping, but the process never took into consideration the end users' perspective. It was at a more corporate-level or "employee profile" and not tailored to our project.

Many EY employees we talked to simply wanted "Salesforce" or SAP instead of a custom EY application. However, Salesforce was too expensive (350k+ user seats), and SAP was too slow and restrictive. The stakeholders we worked with had invested a lot of time and money into the new Lens platform and were continuing to dig the hole deeper regardless of what we found.

In the early stages, the primary stakeholder justifiably expressed their lack of faith in the UX researcher's understanding and engagement in the problem space and project. I did as much as I could to get involved in the research aspects to learn the problem space directly.

Despite the complexity of the issues at hand, we were fortunate to have a new hire on a team who was an expert in the areas of accounting and professional services. He and I had many informative sessions together. While he wasn't dedicated to the project, he was instrumental in shaping our observations into ideas and opportunities.

The PO, UXR and I gathered observations independently— and expected everyone to synthesize, but found out as our executive experience director told me reassuringly "not everyone understands how to do that." I ended up synthesizing a majority of all of our raw notes. It did take time away from ideation, but I found during that time, working with raw data is often time well-spent.

I was able to lean on the executive experience director for support and process—a very intelligent, humble and collaborative coach who I relied on as a sounding board. Together we remained focused on "discovery" and our UX methodologies instead of following the client down their own "rabbit holes." This was one of the best lessons I've learned in recent years.

While I disagree with the method of doing collaborative creative workshops with our stakeholders (I believe it's "UX Lite" or "UX Theater"), I understood its relevance in this particular case. After all, these people were product people at EY, so it may have put them into a more exploratory mindset. Additionally, the executive experience director told me to consider it as another set of input, and we'd still drive our final report how we saw fit— which is good and open-minded advice.

Another challenge was that EY Stakeholders continually pushed for "validation" (not a term I'd use) of their preconcieved / partially built Recommendation Engine. It was a line item in our scope of work, but continually reminded them we were doing "usability testing" and "discovery". The stakeholders had a lot invested in the engine, so we never got a true sense of why it was built without any user input.

Additionally we couldn't use any interviewees names or any personal identifiable information on our computers because of security and GDPR rights. So we had to refer to them as numbers in any file or data we had on our work devices or web applications.
