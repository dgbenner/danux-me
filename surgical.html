Intuitive Surgical

Robotic-Assisted Surgery Training Platform


Intuitive Surgical is a Sunnyvale, California-based company that develops and manufactures robotic surgery products.

Their highest profile product is the 3 to 4-armed Da Vinci Surgical System. It is designed to minimize the effects of surgery to improve clinical outcomes in patients.

Because of the degree of precision and dexterity of the robotic arms and tooling, patients experience very little trauma—and they heal faster and more easily. Overall there is less pain, lower infection risk, reduced blood loss, and fewer complications.

Currently, the best use is for laparoscopic surgery (abdominal or pelvis—with a 1/2 inch incision—sometimes called "keyhole surgery"), as well as lung and esophagus-related surgeries.

In case you were planning on picking one up to make fly-fishing lures or to tie your shoes, the system runs about $1.5 million.

While every surgeon and care network wants better outcomes, the path to becoming a robotic-assisted surgeon requires a high degree of tutorial training, hardware expertise, a firm understanding of their personal technique and how it relates to Da Vinci, as well as simulator time.

The current state of Intuitive Surgical's Learning Platform (the IL 9.0) is a very poor, potentially unusable fully-custom web application and iOS / Android counterparts.

The unfortunate state of the platform has led dedicated-to-learning surgeons and care systems to "go rogue", creating Facebook and YouTube groups for sharing methods and content.


Objective

Lead a product-level discovery to uncover and prioritize usability and feature needs for the robotic-assisted-surgery Intuitive Surgical Learning Platform.

Outcome

After completing the synthesis, I mapped out a set of features designed to immediately enhance the usability of the application. These improvements focus on practical, linear enhancements to learnability, efficiency, memorability, and error reduction.

Since this was a fully custom, hand-coded platform, it lacked high quality UI elements (like Bootstrap or React would provide), such as functional and tested accordions, alerts, dropdowns, mouseover effects etc. These types of features can be implemented with minimal rework, design, involvement of decision-makers—and sometimes they can be as simple as a user story.


Sample of Immediate Impact Features: Adoption, Engagement, and Navigation:

  • Allow learners to provide feedback on content
  • Breadcrumb navigation
  • Consolidate catalogs into a single searchable, filterable index
  • Display learners' progress
  • Enhance / clarify onboarding process
  • Explain the Intuitive Surgical learning methodology / philosophy
  • Implement hover / click states for interaction
  • Introduce peer comparison metrics
  • Offer additional content within courses for tangential learning
  • Pin headers, navigation, and filters
  • Provide links or instructions for connecting to the simulator
  • Utilize icons for wayfinding and recognizing content types
  • Visualize the learning path and goals

Target timeframe: From now to 1-1.5 years


I also introduced a set of more complex features that required further definition but would contribute to a more enjoyable and engaging learning experience. This adaptable set of tactics could evolve as we gather more information about the learners or as new ideas emerge within the collective group. Most of these ideas were generated by me—based on the foundation of my research analysis.


Sample of Deeper Learning Experience Features: Retention, Milestone Completion, and Content Fluency

  • Allow users to create a personalized "glossary" or bookmarks for valuable content
  • Connect learners' real-world events (e.g., completed surgeries, simulator time) to the learning platform for analysis and additional content
  • Enable users to set their own daily notification goals, triggers, or reminders for content and goals
  • Give learners paths forward to third-party content (e.g., YouTube) with Intuitive Surgical commentary / disclaimers
  • Implement "Hot Spot" / hyperlinking within courses to allow tangential learning
  • Include video metadata / indexed transcript / chapters for easier findability
  • Introduce an abstract motivator / metric to show learner progress (like Experience Points)
  • Suggest other available learning paths / skillsets
  • Tie learner's real-world events (e.g., completed surgery, simulator time) back to the learning platform for analysis, additional content
  • Utilize "spaced repetition" of content to stay fluent on learned skills

Target timeframe: 1 year and beyond

Challenges

From the very beginning, I was eager to absorb insights from the research findings and collaborate closely with the human factors engineers and product owners.

While I made significant progress with my interactions with the HFEs, the UX Head did not want me to connect with the product owners directly.

I stressed the importance one-on-one interactions with the stakeholders— which I had emphasized as a critical part of discoveries during my interview process. The UX Head agreed to an introductory email, which unfortunately was postponed multiple times.

Undeterred by the delays, I continued to voice the significance of engaging with the product owners. I believed that these interactions were crucial not only for my clarifications and learning, but also for building social connections, understanding their interests, and identifying any potential conflicts among stakeholders.

In response, the UX Head eventually proposed a group meeting as an initial introduction, a suggestion I reluctantly accepted. My private plan was then to subsequently approach each product owner individually.

A few days later, an interaction designer mentioned "when we do your workshop," revealing that the UX Head had been discussing a workshop with the product owners behind the scenes. I followed up with the UX Head on purpose of this workshop— and how I might want to know the details were I to plan it. Given that we had already received substantial UI-related feedback and that I thought our objectives were aligned, I expressed my concerns to the UX Head, and she said they would want to be part of the solutioning process.

I explained that such a workshop might duplicate efforts and sideline the basic UI challenges we were addressing. I had spent considerable time strategizing and collaborating with her on tactics over the past weeks, and her feedback had been consistently positive.

I felt that delegating the solutioning process to a broad, cross-team workshop could potentially undervalue our work and come across as mere "UX Theater" or "UX Lite".

As an alternative, I proposed have a focused findings reveal session, which I believed could provide valuable insights and aid cross-team collaboration if needed for more visionary features.

Over the next three weeks, I worked closely with the UX Head to shape a workshop plan. Although I vocalized my belief that this effort seemed disproportionate to the simple UI improvements, I remained committed to enhancing the process.

Throughout this process, I made it clear that the direction we were taking diverged from my initial expectations and the notion of an efficient workflow. Despite my efforts, I found it challenging to receive a straightforward explanation for convening a large group of approximately 30 individuals to discuss straightforward UI matters.

Additionally, I expressed it was unfair to the attendees to explain UX a as whole, explain our methodology, reveal findings, and then ask them to participate in ranking or solutioning within the same 3 hours.

In the first 30 minutes of the presentation, a vocal PO expressed her thoughts on UX and the workshop itself: "Why are you doing this? This is our job. Your UX team needs to wait for us to tell you what feature to do—and we'll let you know when we need you to do something."

After a little more hostile discussion between the UX Head and the PO, the PO moved to end the meeting.

The UX Head said she'd regroup—which she did, and concluded we should build a high-fidelity prototype of a fully-functional application that incorporates all of Intuitive Surgical's applications (actual DaVinci devices, surgical system, console, patient cart, learning, simulators, and AI-based solutioning) and present it to the broader product team as an entirely new system from scratch.

I wholeheartedly disagreed with the siloed, Waterfall approach—citing it could take a year or more to even develop anything. (I found out later it's not Waterfall, but more of a "Big Design Up Front" [BDUF] approach.) And that timeframe didn't seem to bother the UX Head. I suggested we tackle the "known knowns" UI-centric issues ourselves and bring the group in to show ad hoc concept models of more visionary features. (Either approach was outside the scope of the discovery project that I was brought on for.)

Contrary to all of this cultural conflict and misunderstanding of the roles of product and UX—and even the product model itself, I had delivered an actionable set of immediate features whose significance couldn't be denied. The larger, visionary features had a clear roadmap for discussion and exploration, all based on the intelligence I had learned through the discovery process and the basic tenets of UX.

Final Analysis

What I thought was a simple improvement process turned out to have cultural dynamics overshadowing the project.

I should have been more clear and unrelenting to do one-on-ones with the stakeholders. They deserve visibility to the process, and I could have learned from their perspectives. I did have a good deal of (although very basic) indirect feedback from them through the decks I'd reviewed.

I'd assumed they would be open to simple usability improvements, and perhaps they were. The vocal PO shut down the discussion.

For good or bad, I typically have blind faith in my leadership within UX until I am proved wrong. I have learned multiple UX strategy methods from true experts in the business. I do have an even higher degree of trust on those learnings, but from situations like this—I need to have even more faith in them and myself. The basic principles of understanding the user and understanding the business needs are not trivial. And at the same time, a product team who doesn't think "visionarily" will only stretch so far on their own. Relationship-building, trust and their knowledge of my discovery process is what is going to advance their thinking. And nothing is more "proof in the pudding" than a tiered, iterative improvement process.

If I were going to rework this project, I'd have kept all the UX strategy to myself, or immediate UX group, meet the stakeholders, and broadly address their needs within the UX strategy as the only or primary deliverable. This discovery project was too far outside their UX maturity level.
